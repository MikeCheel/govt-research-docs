DUI Prevention Program Analysis

Cost-Effectiveness Analysis of a Rideshare-Based Drunk Driving Prevention Program

This document analyzes a rideshare program to prevent drunk driving, where customers pay $1-2 per ride, bars contribute a small amount via a per-drink surcharge, and government subsidies may cover the rest. It addresses the fairness of DUI laws (e.g., rigid 0.08% BAC limit, inaccurate testing) and their economic impact, aiming to reduce DUI arrests, save costs, and free police for serious crimes. Bar contributions are set to maintain profits and enable advertising of affordable rides, leveraging competition.

Key Assumptions

Ride Costs
  • Average ride: $20 (5-10 mile urban ride during nightlife hours)
  • Customer contribution: $1-2 per ride (average $1.50) for affordability
  • Bar contribution: Funded by per-drink surcharge, set to preserve 20-30% profit margins
Program Scenarios
  • Scenario 1: No government intervention (private market: customers + bars + rideshare/philanthropy)
  • Scenario 2: Government subsidies cover remaining costs, funded by alcohol taxes or enforcement savings
DUI Enforcement
  • Annual arrests: 1.1M in the U.S.
  • Cost per arrest: $1,000-$2,000 ($1-2B total)
  • Crash damages: $44B annually
Bar Scenario
  • Customers per night: 100
  • Need rides: 20% (20 customers)
  • Drinks per customer: Average 3 (300 drinks sold)

Fairness of DUI Laws and Economic Impact

Unfair 0.08% BAC Limit
  • Individual variation: BAC varies by weight, metabolism (200-lb man vs. 120-lb woman)
  • Affected arrests: ~20-30% (220,000-330,000) for minimal impairment
  • Enforcement cost: $220-660M annually
  • Personal losses: $1.4-5.5B ($5K-20K per arrest)
Inaccurate Testing
  • Breathalyzer error: ¬±0.01-0.02% margin of error
  • False positives: 10-15% of arrests
  • Cost impact: $100-300M in wrongful arrests
Program Benefits
  • Prevention: $1.50 rides prevent driving near 0.08%
  • Cost avoidance: $1.4-5.5B for 275,000 avoided DUIs
  • Equity: Low fee ensures accessibility for lower-income individuals

Impact on Police and Crime

DUI Reduction Impact
  • Program effectiveness: Similar programs (Road Crew) cut DUIs by 17%
  • Projected reduction: 20% (220,000 arrests)
  • Cost savings: $220-440M
  • Officer hours freed: 110,000-220,000 hours
Crime Clearance Opportunity
  • Current clearance rates: Burglary (14%), Robbery (30%)
  • Potential improvement: Freed officers could focus on serious crimes
  • Limitation: Bureaucracy may limit effective redirection
Fairness Relief
  • Prevention focus: Avoids arrests from rigid 0.08% limits
  • Error reduction: Prevents arrests from faulty breathalyzer tests
  • Financial protection: Saves individuals $5K-20K per avoided arrest

Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

Without Government Subsidies
  • Scale: 50K rides ($1M total, $250K bar contribution at $5/ride)
  • Prevention: ~250 DUIs avoided
  • Enforcement savings: $250-500K
  • Personal cost savings: $1.25-5M
  • Benefit-cost ratio: 1.2-2.5 (better than failed free programs: 0.3-0.6)
With Government Subsidies
  • Same prevention: 250 DUIs avoided
  • Lower private cost: $250K bars + $675K government
  • Benefit-cost ratio: 1.5-3
  • Government cost: $13.5M for 1M rides, offset by enforcement savings

Bar Incentives and Competition

Profitability Maintenance
  • Margin preservation: $0.33-0.67/drink maintains 63-67% profit margins
  • Marketing advantage: Advertise "safe rides for $1.50"
  • Patronage boost: 5-10% increase offsets surcharge costs
Competitive Dynamics
  • Market pressure: Bars without programs lose safety-conscious customers
  • Differentiation: Safety programs become competitive advantage
  • Adoption spread: Competition drives program expansion
Additional Benefits
  • Liability reduction: Lower dram shop risk (overserving liability)
  • Community relations: Enhanced reputation for social responsibility
  • Government support: Ensures program stability and broader participation

Conclusion

A rideshare program with customers paying $1.50, bars contributing $0.33-0.67/drink ($5-10/ride), and optional government subsidies is cost-effective and addresses DUI law unfairness:

Private Market Model
  • Bars fund $5-10/ride via surcharges, with sponsors/rideshare covering shortfalls
  • Saves $275-550M in enforcement and $1.4-5.5B in personal costs
  • Benefit-cost ratio: 1.2-2.5
Government-Supported Model
  • Same surcharges, government covers $13.50/ride ($10M/year for a city)
  • Benefit-cost ratio: 1.5-3, funded by taxes/savings
  • Prevents unfair arrests from 0.08% limits and faulty tests
Key Advantages
  • Bar appeal: Low surcharges enable advertising, boost patronage via competition
  • Police impact: Frees 110,000-220,000 officer hours for serious crimes
  • Market efficiency: Leverages private market and government support

This approach leverages private market efficiency and government support to reduce DUIs, mitigate unfair penalties, and optimize police resources while maintaining bar profitability and encouraging widespread adoption through competitive market forces.

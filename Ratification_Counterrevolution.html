Ratification & Centralization (1787–1790)

From Confederation to Constitution: Underhanded Maneuvers & the Counter‑Movement

This page documents how leading Federalists advocated a strong central government and employed tactics that critics described as underhanded or subversive to secure ratification—paired with Anti‑Federalist responses, quotes, and verifiable links.

Step‑by‑Step Timeline

Tip: Use the filter in the navbar to show entries by actor.

⏳ May 29, 1787 — Delegates; Madison; Randolph (Philadelphia Convention)
What happened (Federalist action)
Convention adopts a strict rule of secrecy and receives the Virginia Plan (a comprehensive blueprint for a stronger national government).
Why it was underhanded
Secrecy insulated deliberations from public scrutiny and pre‑empted incremental amendment by putting a ready‑made centralized blueprint on the table.
Outcome
Set the agenda around replacing (not merely amending) the Articles of Confederation.
Anti‑Federalist response
“The people are entitled to full knowledge of the changes proposed to their government. — Pennsylvania dissenters (Dec. 1787).”
Sources: Rule of Secrecy (Farrand/ConSource) • Virginia Plan (Avalon) • Pennsylvania Minority Dissent
⏳ June 18, 1787 — Alexander Hamilton (Philadelphia Convention)
What happened (Federalist action)
Marathon address proposing life‑tenure executive and senate; praises British ‘energy’ as a model.
Why it was underhanded
Anchoring tactic: by advocating a maximal model bordering on monarchy, Hamilton shifted the Overton window so a very strong but term‑limited central government would feel moderate.
Outcome
Though rejected in form, the debate moved toward embracing ‘energy’ and broad national powers.
Anti‑Federalist response
“[The Constitution] would effect a consolidation of the states. — Brutus I (Oct. 18, 1787).”
Sources: Hamilton Speech (Avalon) • Brutus I
⏳ Sept. 17, 1787 — Benjamin Franklin (Philadelphia Convention)
What happened (Federalist action)
Closing speech urges unanimous signing despite reservations, to present a united front to the public.
Why it was underhanded
Unity pressure: by emphasizing unanimity, dissenting delegates were encouraged to sign despite misgivings, limiting visible alternatives and dampening public opposition.
Outcome
All state delegations present sign; one text goes to the people.
Anti‑Federalist response
“A bill of rights is essential to liberty. — Anti‑Federalist addresses, 1787–88.”
Sources: Franklin Closing Speech
⏳ Oct. 6, 1787 — James Wilson (Philadelphia & Pennsylvania)
What happened (Federalist action)
State House Yard Speech presents the Constitution as balanced yet energetic.
Why it was underhanded
Narrative preemption: a mass meeting and rapid pamphleteering framed public debate before opponents fully organized, branding critics as alarmists.
Outcome
Boosted ratification momentum in PA and other states.
Anti‑Federalist response
“The powers of the consolidated government are immense. — Pennsylvania Minority Dissent (Dec. 18, 1787).”
Sources: Wilson Speech • PA Minority Dissent
⏳ Feb. 6, 1788 — John Hancock; Samuel Adams; Federalists & Opponents (Massachusetts)
What happened (Federalist action)
‘Massachusetts Compromise’: ratify now, recommend a bill of rights and structural amendments.
Why it was underhanded
Back‑room coalition & conditional framing coaxed fence‑sitters to accept ratification first, diluting leverage to demand changes prior to adoption.
Outcome
Ratifies 187–168; model spreads to NH, VA, and NY.
Anti‑Federalist response
“First secure rights, then grant powers. — Anti‑Federalist theme in MA debates.”
Sources: MHS – Ratifying Convention • NCC – Recommended Amendments
⏳ Feb.–Jun. 1788 — NH Federalists; John Langdon; John Sullivan (New Hampshire)
What happened (Federalist action)
Adjourn when votes were lacking; reconvene after momentum built elsewhere.
Why it was underhanded
Delay tactic avoided a public ‘No’ and allowed time for targeted lobbying and shifting public pressure.
Outcome
Ratifies on June 21, 1788 (the 9th state), triggering Article VII.
Anti‑Federalist response
“Adjournment was used to manufacture consent. — Contemporary Anti‑Federalist commentary.”
Sources: NH Ratification (TAH) • Article VII (Avalon)
⏳ 1787–1788 — Hamilton; Madison; Jay (as ‘Publius’) (New York)
What happened (Federalist action)
Coordinate The Federalist essays to argue for energetic national powers.
Why it was underhanded
Elite media campaign: coordinated serial essays framed consolidation as necessary for defense and prosperity, marginalizing Anti‑Federalists as parochial or anti‑union.
Outcome
Created the intellectual case for conditional ratification; NY ratifies after VA & NH.
Anti‑Federalist response
“A free republic cannot succeed over a territory of such immense extent. — Brutus I.”
Sources: Federalist Papers (Avalon) • Brutus I
⏳ July 1788 — NY Convention (mixed) (New York)
What happened (Federalist action)
‘Circular Letter’ urges a second convention to secure amendments post‑ratification.
Why it was underhanded
Split‑the‑difference compromise ratified first (to avoid isolation) while promising later changes—lowering immediate opposition without binding outcomes.
Outcome
NY ratifies; amendment campaign escalates nationally.
Anti‑Federalist response
“Promises of future amendments are no substitute for present securities. — Anti‑Federalists in NY.”
Sources: NY Circular Letter (NCC)
⏳ May 1790 — U.S. Congress; RI leadership (Rhode Island)
What happened (Federalist action)
Non‑Intercourse Act threatens trade sanctions unless RI ratifies.
Why it was underhanded
Economic coercion leveraged federal power to compel a holdout state to join.
Outcome
Rhode Island ratifies on May 29, 1790 as the 13th state.
Anti‑Federalist response
“Coercion proves the danger of consolidation. — Anti‑Federalist pamphleteers.”
Sources: Senate Historical Office – Rhode Island & the Constitution

Anti‑Federalist Responses & Core Themes

  • Brutus I (Oct. 18, 1787) — Warned that the Constitution would ‘effect a consolidation’ and lead to an ‘unalterable, consolidated… government.’ (source)
  • Federal Farmer No. 1 (Nov. 1787) — Objected to indefinite federal powers; urged a bill of rights and protection of state authority. (source)
  • Cato (1787–88) — Argued the proposed executive invited monarchy and that large republics fail to protect liberty. (source)
  • PA Minority Dissent (Dec. 18, 1787) — Documented constraints in the PA convention; argued the plan consolidated power and lacked a bill of rights. (source)
  • Patrick Henry (VA, June 1788) — Attacked the shift from ‘We, the states’ to ‘We, the people’ as the core of consolidation. (source)

Separate Analysis (Counterrevolution Thesis with Links)

The ‘Counterrevolution’ Thesis

Drawing on Rothbardian scholarship, Federalists advanced a centralized blueprint under the rhetoric of crisis and efficiency. The shift from the Articles to a national Constitution is framed as a ‘counterrevolution’ because it replaced a confederation of sovereign states with an energetic national state.

References: Mises: Radical Assault on the Spirit of the Revolution • Mises: Why the 1787 Constitution Did Not Bring Republican Government

Mechanics of Subversion

Tactics included secrecy rules, agenda control (starting with full replacement drafts), narrative preemption via coordinated media, adjournment/delay to avoid ‘No’ votes, conditional ratification promises, and economic coercion—all documented across state proceedings and contemporary pamphlets.

References: Rules of the Convention (Secrecy) • Federalist Papers (Avalon index) • Rhode Island & the Constitution (Senate Historical Office)

Anti‑Federalist Vision

Anti‑Federalists consistently warned of consolidation, standing armies, distant courts, and loss of state sovereignty, demanding a bill of rights and structural limits. Their pressure produced the first ten amendments (Bill of Rights).

References: Brutus I • Federal Farmer • Pennsylvania Minority Dissent
